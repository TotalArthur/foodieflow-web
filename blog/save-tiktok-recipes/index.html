How to Save a Recipe From TikTok (So You Actually Cook It)

Published July 23, 2026 · By Arthur, Founder of FoodieFlow · ~7 min read

It's 11pm, you're three scrolls deep on TikTok, and someone's just tipped an obscene amount of garlic into a pan with total confidence. Thirty seconds later, you've mentally decided you're making that this week.

Then, predictably, nothing happens. The video slides into your Liked tab, filed away next to four hundred other recipes you were absolutely going to cook "one day."

Here's the thing though: that's not a willpower problem. It's a tools problem. TikTok is built for watching, not for cooking from. There's no ingredient list sitting under the video, no method broken into steps, and definitely no shopping list waiting for you at the till. So here's how to actually get a recipe out of your phone and onto your plate, without doing all the tedious bit yourself.

Quick answer: can you properly save a TikTok recipe?

  • Write it out by hand while rewatching the video. Works, but slow.
  • Screen record it and transcribe the recipe later. A bit faster, still entirely manual.
  • Paste the link into an app like FoodieFlow, which pulls the ingredients and method out automatically and drops it straight into your meal plan and shopping list.

You're Not the Only One With a Graveyard of Saved Videos

This isn't just a you problem. A survey from Instacart found that of people who save recipes from social media, only around a third say they always or often actually go on to make them. About one in five say they rarely or never do. Everyone else sits in "sometimes" limbo, which is really just a nicer way of saying "maybe, eventually, probably not."

The bit that makes this worse

Even if you do go back for a saved recipe, the video itself might not still be there. A peer-reviewed study that tracked over 800 popular TikTok videos for a year found more than a third had become inaccessible by the twelve-month mark, deleted, made private, or removed entirely. A save on TikTok is a bookmark, not a backup.

Why Saving a TikTok Recipe Is Harder Than It Should Be

Most recipe apps were built for a different internet. Paprika is genuinely excellent at clipping a recipe from a food blog, but point it at a TikTok link and it has nothing to grab onto, since there's no structured recipe sitting under the video for it to read. Mealime doesn't let you import outside recipes at all, from anywhere. AnyList and Plan to Eat tell much the same story: strong at what they actually do (shopping lists and meal calendars, respectively), but both limited to importing from websites.

None of that is really a knock on those apps. Video-first platforms simply weren't part of the brief when most of them were built, and none of them have caught up since.

Three Ways to Actually Save a TikTok Recipe

Option 1: The Manual Way

If you'd rather not install anything new, here's the process:

  1. Watch the video twice. Once for the vibe, once with a note open to catch ingredients and rough quantities.
  2. Check the caption and comments. A lot of creators drop the full written recipe there, worth checking before you transcribe anything by hand.
  3. Write it out properly. Turn "a good glug of olive oil" into something you can actually shop for.
  4. Build your own shopping list. Cross-check it against what's already in your cupboard, unless you enjoy owning three jars of cumin.

This works. It's also exactly why most TikTok recipes never get cooked: by the time you've done all that, the moment's passed and you're three videos further into your scroll.

The manual way: pause, rewatch, write it all down by hand.

Option 2: Screen Record and Transcribe Later

Some people screen record the video so it's saved locally, then transcribe the recipe whenever they've got five minutes spare. It's a bit faster than rewatching in the app itself, but you're still doing all the translation work by hand: ingredients, quantities, and a shopping list, all typed out yourself, just on your own schedule instead of at 11pm.

The Bigger Pattern Here

Look across the major meal planning apps right now and there's a genuine, obvious gap. Paprika's own users regularly ask for social video import. Mealime's recipe catalogue is closed off entirely, you can't bring your own recipes in at all. AnyList and Plan to Eat are excellent at lists and calendars, but neither touches video import either.

It's less that any of these apps did something wrong, and more that they were built for a different moment. TikTok wasn't really a recipe source when most of them launched, and it shows. For context, recipes are now the single most common type of influencer content worldwide, and a growing share of under-25s use TikTok itself as a search engine for finding food ideas rather than Google. The apps built to organise that content just haven't caught up yet.
