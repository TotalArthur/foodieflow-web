What to Cook With Leftovers: The Ultimate Zero-Waste Guide

Published July 7, 2026 · By Arthur, Founder of FoodieFlow · ~8 min read

You open the fridge and there it is again: half a tub of rice, two sad slices of roast chicken, a heel of bread that's gone a bit hard, and a handful of veg that's not far off the compost bin. None of it looks like "a meal" on its own, so it sits there for another day. Then another. Then it's bin day.

Sound familiar? You're not alone. WRAP, the UK's Waste and Resources Action Programme, estimates that UK households throw away around 4.4 million tonnes of edible food every year, worth roughly £17.5 billion. For an average household of four, that works out to about £1,000 a year, just tossed out. Most of that isn't spoiled food. It's good food that nobody had a plan for.

This guide is that plan. Below you'll find genuinely useful, safe, and honestly quite tasty ideas for the leftovers that pile up in every kitchen: rice, roast chicken, mashed potato, pasta, tired veg, stale bread, and those odd cheese ends nobody wants to bin. We'll also cover the food safety rules that actually matter, because "just heat it up" isn't the whole story.

The Only Two Rules You Actually Need for Leftover Safety

Before the fun part, a quick but important detour. Most food poisoning from leftovers isn't bad luck. It comes down to two mistakes that are easy to avoid once you know about them.

Rule 1: Get it cold, fast

Bacteria multiply fastest in what food safety folks call the "danger zone," roughly between 8°C and 63°C. The UK Food Standards Agency recommends cooling cooked food and getting it into the fridge within two hours of cooking. Rice is even more time-sensitive: chill it within an hour if you can. Splitting a big batch into smaller, shallow containers helps it cool faster, so don't just shove the whole pot in as one solid block.

Rule 2: Reheat it properly, and only once

When you do reheat leftovers, they need to be steaming hot all the way through, not just warm on the outside. FSA home guidance points to around 70°C for two minutes at the core (commercial kitchens in Scotland work to a stricter 82°C). Stir food partway through if you're using a microwave, since microwaves heat unevenly and can leave cold spots where bacteria survive. Once something's been reheated, don't reheat what's left again. Eat it or bin it.

As a general rule, most cooked leftovers keep for about two days in a fridge running at 5°C or below. If you know you won't get to something in time, freeze it instead of hoping for the best. Freezing doesn't kill bacteria, but it stops it multiplying, which genuinely buys you weeks instead of days.

Leftover Rice

What to Cook With Leftover Rice

Cold, leftover rice is honestly better for frying than rice you've just cooked. The grains dry out a little and separate instead of turning gluey. A few ways to use it up:

  • Fried rice. Get a wok or wide pan properly hot, add a splash of oil, then add the rice with whatever veg, egg, or leftover meat you've got going spare. Keep it moving, and finish with soy sauce and a touch of sesame oil.
  • Rice pudding. Heat the rice gently with milk (or a tin of condensed milk for a sweeter version), sugar, and a bit of cinnamon or vanilla. Stir over low heat until it thickens. Ten minutes, genuinely.
  • Rice cakes or arancini-style bites. Mix cold rice with a beaten egg and grated cheese, form into small patties or balls, coat in breadcrumbs, and shallow fry until golden. Great dipped in leftover pasta sauce.
  • Stuffed peppers. Halve a bell pepper, pack it with rice mixed with whatever's around (cooked mince, beans, chopped veg), top with cheese, and bake until the pepper softens.

Safety note: chill rice fast, ideally within the hour, and only reheat it once. Rice left out at room temperature can carry a bacteria called Bacillus cereus, and reheating won't necessarily deal with it. When in doubt, bin it.

A ten-minute rice pudding is one of the easiest ways to use up leftover rice.

Leftover Roast Chicken or Turkey

What to Cook With Leftover Roast Chicken or Turkey

  • Quick soup. Simmer the carcass (if you kept it) or just the meat with stock, a chopped onion, carrot, and celery for 20 to 30 minutes. Add noodles or leftover rice near the end.
  • Quesadillas or wraps. Shred the meat, toss with leftover sauce or salsa and some cheese, fold into a tortilla, and fry until crisp.
  • Chicken salad. Chopped meat, mayo, a squeeze of lemon, and something crunchy like celery or apple. Good in a sandwich or on its own.
  • A simple curry. Fry onion, garlic, and ginger, stir in curry powder or paste, add a tin of tomatoes or coconut milk, then stir the shredded meat through at the end so it doesn't dry out further.

Shredded roast chicken stirred through a simple curry base, right at the end so it stays juicy.

Leftover Mashed Potato

What to Cook With Leftover Mashed Potato

  • Potato cakes. Mix mash with an egg and a handful of flour, shape into patties, and fry in a little butter until crisp on both sides.
  • Shepherd's pie topping. If you've also got leftover cooked mince or lentils, this is the obvious move. Layer mash over the filling, dot with butter, and bake until golden.
  • Gnocchi. Mix mash with flour and an egg, roll into ropes, cut into small pillows, boil until they float, then toss in butter or sauce.
  • Bubble and squeak. Fry mash together with any leftover cooked veg (cabbage, sprouts, carrots) until it's got a good crust on the outside. A classic for a reason.
Leftover Pasta

What to Cook With Leftover Pasta

  • Frittata. Whisk eggs with a splash of milk, stir through cold pasta and any bits of cheese or veg, pour into a hot, oiled pan and cook low and slow until set, finishing under the grill if needed.
  • Pasta bake. Toss cold pasta with a bit more sauce, top with cheese, and bake until bubbling. Works with almost any leftover sauce you've got.
  • Pasta salad. Cold pasta with olive oil, a squeeze of lemon, chopped veg, and whatever cheese or cured meat is lurking in the fridge. Great for lunch the next day.
Leftover Veg

What to Cook With Leftover Veg (Whatever You've Got)

This is the one people struggle with most, because it's rarely just one vegetable. The trick is to stop treating veg as single ingredients and start treating it as a category.

  • Soup. Almost any combination of veg, simmered in stock until soft and blended, becomes a decent soup. A tin of beans or lentils adds body.
  • Stir-fry. High heat, a bit of oil, harder veg like carrots in first, softer veg like spinach added later, soy sauce and garlic to finish.
  • Roasted veg tacos or wraps. Toss slightly tired veg in oil and roast until caramelised (this hides a multitude of sins), then pile into a tortilla with whatever sauce is going.
  • Frittata or omelette. Same principle as the pasta version above. Veg plus egg plus cheese is nearly always a meal.

Whatever veg is closest to going off becomes the base of the bowl.

Stale Bread

What to Cook With Stale Bread

  • French toast. Whisk egg with milk and a bit of cinnamon, dunk the bread, and fry in butter. Stale bread actually works better here since it soaks up the mixture without falling apart.
  • Croutons. Cube the bread, toss in oil and whatever herbs you like, and bake until crisp. Keeps for weeks in a jar.
  • Panzanella. Toasted, torn bread mixed with tomatoes, cucumber, red onion, olive oil, and vinegar. Let it sit for 20 minutes so the bread soaks up the dressing.
  • Bread pudding. Layer torn bread in a dish, pour over a custard of egg, milk, sugar, and vanilla, and bake until set.

Stale bread makes better French toast than fresh, since it soaks up the custard without falling apart.

Leftover Cheese

What to Cook With Leftover Cheese Odds and Ends

  • Mac and cheese. Whatever cheese you've got, melted into a basic white sauce (butter, flour, milk) and poured over cooked pasta. Two or three types mixed together usually beats any single one.
  • Quiche or crustless frittata. Any cheese, any leftover veg or meat, poured into egg and baked, with or without a pastry case.
  • A cheese board with a twist. Sometimes the answer is just: put it on a board with crackers, a bit of chutney, and call it dinner. Not everything needs to be a recipe.

Freezing Leftovers: What Actually Freezes Well

Freezing is a genuinely good habit to build, not just a last resort. A few notes worth knowing:

Freezes well

  • Soups, stews and curries
  • Cooked rice
  • Bolognese and most sauces
  • Bread
  • Most cooked meat

Doesn't freeze well

  • Dishes with a lot of raw potato (goes grainy)
  • Cream-based sauces (can split)
  • Salad veg like lettuce or cucumber (goes watery)
  • Boiled eggs (turn rubbery)

Doing it properly: portion food before freezing rather than as one giant block, and label it with the date. Defrost in the fridge, not on the counter, and once defrosted use it within 24 hours, reheating only once.

Frozen food doesn't go "off" the way fresh food does, since freezing pauses bacterial growth rather than killing it, but quality does drop over time. As a rough guide, most cooked meals taste best used within two to three months, even though they'd technically still be safe well beyond that.

Frequently asked questions

How long do leftovers last in the fridge?

Most cooked leftovers are good for about two days when stored at 5°C or below. Rice is more sensitive and best eaten within a day.

Can you reheat leftovers more than once?

It's best not to. Every time food cools and reheats, bacteria get another chance to multiply. Reheat once, and finish what you've reheated rather than saving it again for later.

Is it safe to eat cold leftovers straight from the fridge?

Generally yes, as long as they were cooked and cooled properly in the first place. It's specifically reheating that needs to bring food back up to steaming hot throughout, not eating it cold.

Can you freeze rice?

Yes. Cool it quickly, portion it, and freeze. Defrost in the fridge and reheat until steaming hot, only once.

What leftovers should you never eat?

Anything left out at room temperature for more than two hours, anything with visible mould (unless it's a food where that's normal, like a hard cheese rind), or anything that smells or looks off. When in doubt, bin it. It's not worth the risk.
